Spain by Camper: Sun, Freedom, and Important Road Rules

The Iberian Peninsula on Four Wheels

Spain attracts van life fans with its climate and great infrastructure. To keep your trip stress-free, you need to understand the local rules—especially the difference between parking and camping.

Pernocta vs. Camping: the key concept

The most important word is “pernocta”: sleeping in your vehicle without setting up anything outside. If your vehicle stays on its wheels, doesn’t block traffic, and you don’t put out gear (awning, chairs, table), many places treat it as regular parking. The moment you expand outside, it can be considered camping and may be fined outside designated areas.

Wild sleeping—how to do it wisely

Spain isn’t uniform: some regions are more tolerant, while tourist hotspots can be strict. Three practical rules that reduce risk:

  • Respect signs (no parking/no overnight) – the most common reason for fines.
  • Leave no trace – no trash, no noise, no outdoor “camp setup”.
  • Choose spots for sleep, not for parties – calm, low-traffic locations help avoid trouble.

Water access and service points

Don’t wait until you’re “running on fumes” with water. In Spain you’ll often find:

  • Fuentes (public taps/fountains): common in towns and cities—carry a tap adapter and a short hose.
  • Área de autocaravanas: camper areas that often offer fresh water and dumping points (sometimes paid).
  • Campsites: every few days it’s worth doing a full reset—shower, laundry, proper service.

When planning your route through Andalusia or Costa Brava, remember to monitor fuel costs and highway tolls. Use Travel Planner App to manage your budget in euros.

What to see (and what to avoid)

  • Andalusia off-season: great weather, iconic cities, fewer crowds.
  • Northern coast & Atlantic areas: greener landscapes, cooler temperatures, often quieter.
  • Scenic routes & parks: prefer official parking areas with clear rules.

Avoid: overcrowded beach promenades in peak season (more restrictions and checks), entering protected zones without knowing the rules, and overnighting in conflict-prone areas (party spots, loud station parkings, crowded tourist lots).

Tip: Always keep a “Plan B” overnight (a campsite or área). It’s the simplest way to make Spain feel easy.
