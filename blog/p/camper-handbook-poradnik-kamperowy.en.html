Camper Handbook: Everything You Need to Know Before Your First Trip

Why every first-timer needs a camper handbook before hitting the road

Your first camper trip is one of those moments when excitement and mild panic arrive together. A camper handbook won't replace experience — but it can make sure your first journey is remembered for stunning views rather than costly mistakes. This guide collects everything you genuinely need to know before you turn the key and head off into the unknown.

Choosing your vehicle: motorhome, campervan or converted bus?

Before you plan any route, you need to decide what you're driving. The vehicle determines comfort, costs and parking possibilities.

  • Factory motorhome: Road-ready with a built-in bathroom, kitchen and power system. Comfortable but expensive to buy and run. Higher fuel consumption, harder to park in cities.
  • Campervan (converted van): The sweet spot. More manoeuvrable than a motorhome, easier to park. You can buy one ready-built or convert it yourself — the latter is cheaper and gives you full control over the layout.
  • Converted bus or minibus: Large vehicles, often high-roofed. Lots of conversion space, but difficult on narrow streets and forest tracks.

For your first adventure a rented motorhome or campervan works best — before spending serious money on your own vehicle, find out whether this travel style actually suits you.

Essential equipment — your starting checklist

Every good camper handbook starts with an equipment list. Without these you're heading for trouble:

  • Electrical system: A secondary battery (ideally LiFePO4) or a solar panel. Without power you have no lighting, no fridge and no charging your devices.
  • Compressor fridge: Absorption fridges are cheaper but consume far more power and cool less effectively. For longer trips a compressor fridge pays for itself.
  • Burner and cookware: A camping burner or gas hob. A compact cookware set for single-burner cooking — lightweight, compact, sufficient.
  • Water tanks: Fresh water and grey water. Minimum 20 litres in reserve — you won't always find somewhere to top up on the road.
  • Extended first aid kit: Standard car first aid kit plus anti-diarrhoea medicine, antiseptic, elastic bandages.
  • Basic tools: Spare wheel (and the ability to change it), spanner set, mounting tape, pump.
  • Heating: Even in summer mountain nights are cold. A gas or diesel heater (Webasto/Eberspächer) is standard in serious builds.

Paperwork and documents — the non-negotiables

Paperwork sounds dull but a fine or a border hold-up won't be dull at all.

  • Correct driving licence category: Most motorhomes under 3.5 tonnes require category B. Heavier vehicles need C1 or C.
  • Third-party and comprehensive insurance: Check that comprehensive cover includes foreign travel. Some policies require an extension.
  • Green card: Essential for travel outside the EU. Required at checks in many countries.
  • European Health Insurance Card (EHIC): Free, issued by your national health service. Get it before every trip to EU countries.
  • Fire extinguisher and warning triangle: Required by law in most European countries. In some countries (France, Austria) reflective vests for every passenger are also mandatory.

Route planning — how not to overload your schedule

The biggest mistake first-time camper drivers make? Planning too many kilometres per day. Driving a large vehicle is more tiring than a regular car. Adopt the rule: a maximum of 250–300 km per day, and less is more.

A good camper route has several features: planned overnight spots (campsite or car park), water fill-up points and grey water disposal points built in, a few backup options in case plans change.

Parking and overnight stays — three basic options

  • Full-service campsite: Power, water, showers, often WiFi. More expensive (20–50 euros/night) but comfortable. Ideal for longer stays.
  • Motorhome parking area (aire): Especially popular in France, Germany and Portugal. Often free or 5–10 euros. Water point and waste disposal. No electricity.
  • Wild overnight: Legal in parts of Europe (Scandinavia, parts of Germany and Poland). Free but requires knowledge of local rules. Golden rule: leave no trace.

Safety — what you must never forget

Safety in a camper is a topic for its own article, but a few rules are absolutely essential:

  • Never use a gas burner with windows closed — risk of carbon monoxide poisoning.
  • A CO detector and smoke detector are a requirement, not an option.
  • When sleeping in isolated spots — let someone close know your location.
  • Don't leave valuable equipment visible through the windows.

Summary
